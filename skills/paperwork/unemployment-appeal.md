HOW TO APPEAL A DENIED UNEMPLOYMENT CLAIM

Steps:
1. Get the denial decision in writing. You should receive a "Notice of Determination" by mail. If you applied online, check your account. Do not proceed without reading the exact reason for denial.
2. Find the appeal deadline. This is the most important step. Unemployment appeal windows are short — typically 10 to 30 days from the date on the determination notice, not the date you received it. Missing the deadline in most states means your appeal is automatically barred, regardless of merit.
3. Read the denial reason. Common reasons: (a) you were fired "for cause" (misconduct), (b) you quit voluntarily without good cause, (c) you're not available for work, (d) you're not actively looking for work, (e) you earned too little to qualify. Each requires different evidence.
4. File your appeal. Instructions are on the determination notice. Most states accept appeals: online, by mail, by fax, or in person at a workforce/unemployment office. Use whatever method creates a paper trail. Write "APPEAL" clearly on all documents.
5. In your appeal statement, write what happened in your own words. Be factual and specific. If you were fired, state why you believe it was not misconduct. If you quit, state why you had "good cause" (unsafe conditions, harassment, medical necessity, following a spouse to a required relocation — these often qualify).
6. Gather evidence: termination letter, performance reviews, HR emails, doctor's notes, pay stubs, anything relevant to the denial reason. Bring all of it.
7. A hearing will be scheduled, usually by phone. You will receive notice with a date, time, and call-in number. This is your opportunity to present your case to an appeals referee (a neutral hearing officer, not your employer's representative).
8. At the hearing: answer questions directly and briefly. State facts. Refer to your documents (have them in front of you). If your employer is also on the call, you can ask them questions after they speak. You are allowed to have a witness.
9. A written decision will be mailed to you, usually within 2–4 weeks.
10. If you lose the hearing: you have a right to a second-level appeal (a board review), then a court appeal. The standards get stricter at each level. However, many claims are won at the initial hearing that were denied on the initial determination — it is worth pursuing.

Notes:
- Continue filing weekly certifications while your appeal is pending. If you win, you will be paid for all weeks you certified. If you stop certifying, you may not be able to collect those weeks retroactively.
- "Misconduct" has a specific legal meaning in unemployment law — it's generally intentional, willful, or grossly negligent behavior. Simple mistakes, performance issues, or personality conflicts usually don't meet the legal definition.
- You do not need a lawyer for the initial hearing, but some legal aid organizations assist with unemployment appeals for free. Search "[your state] legal aid unemployment."
- States vary significantly in how they define "good cause to quit." Research your state's specific standards.
- If you were laid off, you generally qualify unless the employer challenges it. Most challenges fail.
