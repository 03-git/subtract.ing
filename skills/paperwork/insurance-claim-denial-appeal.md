HOW TO APPEAL A DENIED INSURANCE CLAIM

Steps:
1. Get the denial in writing. If you received it verbally, call and request a written Explanation of Benefits (EOB) or denial letter. You cannot appeal what you don't have in writing.
2. Read the denial reason carefully. Common reasons: (a) not covered under your policy, (b) service deemed "not medically necessary," (c) out-of-network provider, (d) pre-authorization not obtained, (e) billing code error, (f) missed deadline. Each requires a different approach.
3. Pull your policy. Find the section covering what was denied. Read the exact language. Insurers sometimes deny claims that are actually covered — the denial letter may misstate or misapply your policy.
4. Request your complete claim file. You have the right to request this from the insurer. It will include all documents they used to make the decision, including any internal medical reviews.
5. Identify your appeal deadlines. Health insurance (employer plans): at least 180 days from denial under federal law (ERISA). Marketplace plans: similar. Auto and home: check your policy — often 30–60 days. Do not miss this.
6. Write your internal appeal letter. Address it to the appeals/grievance department. Include: your name, policy number, claim number, the denial reason, and your argument for why the claim should be paid. Attach supporting documents (medical records for health, repair estimates for auto/home, photos, expert letters).
7. For "not medically necessary" health denials: get a letter from your treating doctor stating the medical necessity, citing clinical guidelines (like those from the relevant medical specialty society).
8. Submit by certified mail and keep copies of everything. Note the date.
9. Wait for the internal appeal decision (required within 30–60 days for health under federal law; varies for others).
10. If internal appeal is denied: for health insurance, you have the right to an External Review — an independent organization (not the insurer) reviews your case. For marketplace plans, this is free. Request external review within 4 months of the internal denial. For auto/home, consider filing a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance — this alone often triggers reconsideration.

Notes:
- For health insurance, the external review process has overturned millions of denials. It is worth doing.
- Billing code errors are extremely common. Before appealing, call your provider and ask them to verify the codes submitted — sometimes resubmitting a corrected claim is all that's needed.
- Your state's Department of Insurance (insurance commissioner's office) is free to contact and can pressure insurers. Filing a complaint costs nothing.
- For ERISA employer plans, if external review fails, your only recourse is federal court — consult an ERISA attorney who works on contingency.
- Document every phone call: date, time, name of representative, what was said.
