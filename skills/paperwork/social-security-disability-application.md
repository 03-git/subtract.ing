HOW TO APPLY FOR SOCIAL SECURITY DISABILITY (SSDI/SSI)

Steps:
1. Understand the two programs. SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance): based on your work history and Social Security taxes paid — you need enough "work credits" (roughly 5 years of work in the last 10 years for most adults). SSI (Supplemental Security Income): based on financial need, not work history — low income and assets required. You can apply for both simultaneously.
2. Apply as soon as you believe you qualify. The process takes 3–6 months for initial decisions, often longer. SSDI back pay is calculated from your application date (minus a 5-month waiting period). Every month you delay is a month of potential back pay you lose.
3. Three ways to apply: (a) Online at ssa.gov/disability, (b) by phone at 1-800-772-1213, (c) in person at your local Social Security office (find it at ssa.gov/locator). Online is fastest for most people.
4. Gather your documents before starting. You will need: Social Security number, birth certificate or proof of age, proof of citizenship or immigration status, W-2s and tax returns for the past year, medical records (names and addresses of all doctors, hospitals, clinics you've seen for your condition), employment history for the last 15 years (job titles, duties, start/end dates), bank account information for direct deposit. For SSI: also proof of income, resources, living arrangements.
5. Complete the application. Describe how your condition limits your ability to work — be specific and honest about your worst days, not your best days. The standard is: can you perform any substantial gainful work, not just your previous job.
6. Complete the Adult Function Report (SSA-787). This is a separate form where you describe your daily activities. Be specific: "I can walk one block before pain requires me to stop," not "I have trouble walking." Vague answers hurt claims.
7. Get your doctors to submit records. Social Security will contact your providers directly, but this is slow. Provide a signed release, and proactively ask your doctors to submit complete records and a written opinion on your functional limitations.
8. Expect a denial on the first application. Approximately 67% of initial applications are denied. This is not the end — the appeal process (reconsideration, then hearing before an Administrative Law Judge) is where most approvals happen.
9. If denied: file for reconsideration within 60 days. If denied again, request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ). ALJ hearings have a significantly higher approval rate. A disability attorney or advocate (they work on contingency — no upfront cost, they take a percentage of back pay capped by law at $7,200) can significantly help at this stage.
10. Continue to document your condition throughout the process. Keep records of doctor visits, hospitalizations, medications, and how your condition affects daily life. This documentation supports your case at every level.

Notes:
- The "5-month waiting period" for SSDI means benefits start 6 months after your established disability onset date, not your application date.
- For SSI, the asset limit is $2,000 for an individual ($3,000 for a couple) — this includes bank accounts but excludes your primary home and one car.
- Mental health conditions, chronic pain, and non-visible disabilities are fully eligible — but require thorough medical documentation. Regular treatment records are essential.
- Children can qualify for SSI based on the parent/guardian's income and the child's disability.
- Disability attorneys typically take 25% of back pay up to the $7,200 federal cap. No back pay, no fee. They are generally worth engaging by the ALJ hearing stage.
- Medicare eligibility (for SSDI recipients) begins 24 months after disability benefits start — this is a long gap. Look into your state's Medicaid program as a bridge.
