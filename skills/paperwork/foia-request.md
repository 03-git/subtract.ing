HOW TO REQUEST GOVERNMENT RECORDS (FOIA)

Steps:
1. Identify which law applies. Federal agencies: the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). State/local agencies: each state has its own public records law (often called "sunshine laws" or open records acts). For state agencies, search "[your state] public records request."
2. Identify the right agency. Records are held by the agency that created or received them. The FBI holds FBI records. A state DMV holds driving records. A city police department holds local police records. Sending your request to the wrong place delays everything.
3. Find the agency's FOIA office or public records contact. Most federal agencies list a FOIA requester service center on their website. For state/local, call the main number and ask for the records custodian or public records officer.
4. Write your request. You don't need a reason. Include: your name and contact info, a specific description of the records you want (dates, names, subjects, document types — the more specific, the faster), and whether you want digital or paper copies.
5. State your requester category if asking for a fee waiver. Media, educational, and nonprofit requesters often get reduced fees. If you're requesting records about yourself, say so — personal records requests often have no search fees under the Privacy Act.
6. Send the request. Email is accepted by most federal agencies and many state agencies. Certified mail is safest for creating a paper trail. Some agencies have online portals (FOIA.gov lists federal portals).
7. Note the date sent. Federal agencies must acknowledge receipt within 20 business days and respond within 20 business days, though complex requests routinely take months. State deadlines vary (5–10 business days is common).
8. Track your request. Federal agencies must give you a tracking number. If they miss their deadline, send a follow-up letter citing the date of your original request and the statutory deadline.
9. If records are withheld or redacted: the agency must cite the specific FOIA exemption (there are 9) for each withheld item. You have the right to appeal the withholding administratively within the agency.
10. If the internal appeal fails: you can file suit in federal district court (for federal agencies) or in your state court. This is uncommon but it works — courts frequently order records released.

Notes:
- Specificity dramatically increases speed and decreases cost. "All documents ever created about me" will get you a fee estimate of thousands of dollars. "All emails mentioning [your name] sent between January and March 2025 in the [specific office]" is tractable.
- Requesting your own records from federal agencies is governed by both FOIA and the Privacy Act. Use both as the basis for your request.
- Many FOIA requests are fulfilled within weeks if the scope is narrow. Don't assume it will take years.
- MuckRock.com is a free service that helps draft and track FOIA requests if you want a guided process.
- Agencies can charge for search time and duplication. Request a fee waiver upfront, citing your reason (personal use, public interest, etc.). Many agencies waive fees for small requests regardless.
