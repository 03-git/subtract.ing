HOW TO FILE IN SMALL CLAIMS COURT

Steps:
1. Confirm the dollar limit for your state. Limits range from $2,500 (Kentucky) to $25,000 (Tennessee). Most states are $5,000–$10,000. You cannot sue for more than the limit — you would have to waive the excess or file in a higher court.
2. Confirm you have a valid claim. You must be suing someone who: owes you money, damaged your property, broke a contract with you, or wrongfully kept something from you. Small claims court is for money — it cannot force someone to do something.
3. Confirm the defendant is within the court's jurisdiction. Generally, you sue in the county/district where: the defendant lives or does business, the contract was signed, or the incident occurred.
4. Gather your evidence before filing: contracts, receipts, invoices, photos, text messages, emails, demand letters (important — courts like to see you tried to resolve it first), and any witnesses.
5. Go to the small claims court clerk's office. This is usually in the county courthouse. Tell them you want to file a small claims complaint. They will give you a form.
6. Fill out the form. You are the "plaintiff." The person you're suing is the "defendant." Write clearly: what happened, when, and exactly how much money you are owed and why. Bring the defendant's full legal name and current address — this is required.
7. Pay the filing fee. Usually $30–$100, depending on the amount claimed. Some courts waive fees if you're low income — ask the clerk about a fee waiver form.
8. The court will schedule a hearing date, usually 30–70 days out. You will be given a court date.
9. The defendant must be served (officially notified). In many states the court does this by mail. In others, you must arrange it — via a process server ($50–$100) or the sheriff's office ($25–$75). The clerk will tell you what's required.
10. On your hearing date: arrive early, dress plainly and professionally. Bring two copies of all your evidence (one for the judge, one for yourself). Speak to the judge, not the other party. State facts, not emotions. Show your documents. Ask for the specific dollar amount you're owed.

Notes:
- Most states do not allow attorneys in small claims court — this levels the field.
- Bring a written timeline of events to refer to. Nerves are normal; a written outline helps.
- If the defendant doesn't show up, you will likely win by default. If you don't show up, your case is dismissed.
- Winning a judgment doesn't mean you'll automatically get paid. If the defendant ignores the judgment, you can return to the court clerk to garnish their wages or bank account, or place a lien on their property. This is a separate process.
- Some states allow remote/video hearings — ask the clerk.
- State-specific small claims limits and procedures change. Verify current rules with your court clerk before filing.
