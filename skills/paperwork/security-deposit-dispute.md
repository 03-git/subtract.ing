HOW TO GET YOUR SECURITY DEPOSIT BACK

Steps:
1. Know your state's deadline. Every state has a law requiring landlords to return the deposit (and an itemized list of deductions) within a specific number of days after move-out — typically 14 to 30 days. Look up "[your state] security deposit return deadline."
2. Confirm your forwarding address was given. The landlord needs your new address to mail the check. If you didn't give it in writing, send a letter or email immediately with your forwarding address and the date you moved out.
3. Wait out the deadline. Do not contact the landlord constantly. Let the clock run.
4. If the deadline passes with no check and no itemized list: many states allow you to sue for 2–3x the deposit amount as a penalty, plus attorney's fees. This is a strong legal position. Document that the deadline passed.
5. If you receive an itemized list with deductions you dispute: review each deduction. "Normal wear and tear" (small nail holes, carpet wear from use, faded paint) cannot legally be charged to you in any state. Repairs for damage you caused can be charged.
6. Gather your evidence: move-in inspection report, move-out photos (timestamped), your termination letter, and any receipts for repairs you made yourself.
7. Write a demand letter. State: the amount owed, that you vacated on [date], that the deposit was $X, that deductions for [specific items] are improper because [reason], and that you demand the full/partial refund within 10 days or will file in small claims court. Send certified mail with return receipt.
8. Wait 10–14 days for a response.
9. If no response or refusal: file in small claims court (see small-claims-court-filing.md). In most states, security deposit cases are among the strongest cases a tenant can bring. Bring all photos, the lease, the demand letter, and proof it was received.
10. If you win: the court will issue a judgment. If the landlord doesn't pay, you can garnish wages or bank accounts through the court clerk's office.

Notes:
- Move-in inspection reports are critical. If you never received one or never signed one, document that in your demand letter — it weakens the landlord's ability to claim pre-existing damage was yours.
- Some states (California, New York, Massachusetts) have particularly tenant-friendly deposit laws with steep penalties.
- If the landlord claims you owe back rent, that's a separate issue from the deposit. They cannot legally offset the deposit against alleged rent debts in many states without a court judgment.
- Keep the envelope of anything mailed to you — the postmark matters to prove when they sent it.
- Small claims court filing fees are typically $30–$100. You can often recover this in your judgment.
