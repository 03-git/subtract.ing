HOW TO DISPUTE A DEBT COLLECTOR CONTACT

Steps:
1. Do not ignore the contact, but also do not immediately pay or admit the debt. Both inaction and hasty payment can hurt you. Take a breath and gather information first.
2. Write down when and how you were contacted, what was said or written, and the collector's name, company name, and any account number they provided.
3. Identify what type of dispute this is. (a) You don't recognize this debt at all — it may be a case of mistaken identity, identity theft, or a fully paid/discharged debt. (b) You recognize the debt but dispute the amount or the collector's right to collect it. (c) The debt is real but is past the statute of limitations (the legal window for suing you over it — varies by state and debt type, typically 3–6 years). (d) The collector is behaving illegally (calling at illegal hours, threatening arrest, calling your workplace after you've asked them not to).
4. If you received a written notice: you have 30 days from receipt to dispute the debt in writing and demand verification. After this window you can still dispute, but you lose certain automatic protections. (This is the validation window — do not let it pass.)
5. Write a dispute letter. Include: your name and address, the account number they referenced, a clear statement that you dispute this debt (and the reason — don't recognize it, wrong amount, wrong person, paid already, past statute of limitations, etc.), and a demand that they cease collection activity until they provide verification.
6. Send by certified mail with return receipt. Keep a copy of the letter and the green return card.
7. Once the collector receives your dispute: they must stop all collection activity until they send you verification of the debt — typically a copy of the original agreement or a statement showing the debt. If they cannot verify it, they must stop collecting.
8. Review whatever they send. Check: Does the debt match your records? Is the amount correct? Is the original creditor who you expect? Is it actually yours? If you were a victim of identity theft, file an FTC identity theft report at identitytheft.gov and include it in your dispute.
9. If the collector continues contacting you after receiving your dispute, or engages in prohibited practices (threatening jail, calling before 8am or after 9pm, calling your employer after being told not to, using profane language): file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) at consumerfinance.gov/complaint and with your state attorney general. These are violations of the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA).
10. You can sue a collector who violates the FDCPA in small claims or federal court — and recover up to $1,000 per lawsuit plus actual damages and attorney's fees. Document every violation.

Notes:
- Paying a debt that is past the statute of limitations can legally restart the clock in some states, giving the collector renewed ability to sue you. Know your state's statute of limitations before paying old debts.
- Disputing a debt does not make it go away if it's real and valid. It forces the collector to prove it. If they prove it, the dispute is resolved and collection can resume.
- This procedure covers third-party debt collectors (collection agencies). If the original creditor (the bank, hospital, etc.) is contacting you directly, the FDCPA doesn't fully apply, though many states have equivalent laws that do.
- A debt appearing on your credit report is a separate issue from collection contact. You can dispute credit report entries directly with the credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA).
- "Pay for delete" agreements (where a collector agrees to remove a negative item from your credit report in exchange for payment) are not guaranteed to work — the bureaus don't have to honor them — but some collectors will do it. Get it in writing before paying.
