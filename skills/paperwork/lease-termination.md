HOW TO END A LEASE (EARLY OR AT TERM)

Steps:
1. Read your lease. Find the section titled "Termination," "Notice," or "Early Termination." Write down: (a) how many days notice you must give, (b) whether there's an early termination fee, (c) whether notice must be written and how it must be delivered.
2. Check your state's tenant rights law. Many states override lease terms — some require only 20–30 days notice regardless of what the lease says. Search "[your state] notice to vacate law."
3. Calculate your move-out date. If you're ending at term, count backward from your lease end date by the required notice period. If ending early, your move-out date is negotiable, but fees may apply.
4. Write the termination letter. Include: your name, address, unit number, the date you're writing, your intended move-out date, and a line stating you are providing notice per the lease and/or state law. Sign it.
5. Deliver the letter. Use the method your lease specifies. If it says certified mail, use certified mail with return receipt. If it allows email, email AND send a paper copy. Keep proof of delivery.
6. If breaking early and your lease allows it: pay the early termination fee (usually 1–2 months rent) along with the letter, or confirm in writing when you'll pay it.
7. If breaking early and your lease does NOT have a termination clause: you may still terminate, but the landlord can pursue unpaid rent until they re-rent the unit. In most states, they're legally required to try to re-rent (this is called the "duty to mitigate"). Document this.
8. Do a walkthrough. Before handing keys back, do a joint inspection with the landlord. Take timestamped photos of every room, every wall, appliances, and fixtures. Get a written move-out checklist signed by both parties if possible.
9. Get written confirmation of your lease termination. This can be an email reply, a signed letter, or a form from your landlord. Keep it permanently.
10. Return keys on or before move-out date. Get a receipt. Note the exact date and time.

Notes:
- Military members can break any lease penalty-free with 30 days notice under the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA). Attach your deployment/PCS orders.
- Domestic violence survivors can terminate leases early in all 50 states with documentation — laws vary on what's required.
- If the landlord has violated the lease (no heat, no water, mold, harassment), you may have grounds for "constructive eviction" — you can leave without penalty, but document everything first.
- "Verbal notice" is nearly useless legally. Always write it down.
- Some states require notice be given by the 1st of the month regardless of when in the month you're writing. Know your state's rule.
