HOW TO RESPOND TO AN IRS LETTER

Steps:
1. Do not panic. The majority of IRS letters are automated notices about a specific, narrow issue — a math discrepancy, a missing form, a payment not posted — not an investigation or audit.
2. Read the letter fully. Find the notice number — it's in the top right corner, formatted as "CP" followed by numbers (e.g., CP2000, CP14, CP501) or "LTR" followed by numbers. This tells you exactly what the issue is.
3. Look up your notice number. The IRS website lists every notice type with a plain-English explanation. Search "IRS notice [your number]" or go to irs.gov/notices. Read what it means before doing anything else.
4. Note the response deadline. IRS letters have deadlines, usually 30–60 days from the date on the letter (not the date you received it). If you're close to the deadline, act immediately or call the number on the letter to request more time.
5. Determine whether you agree or disagree with the notice.
6. If you agree and owe money: pay online at irs.gov/payments, by check (make it out to "U.S. Treasury," write your SSN and tax year in the memo), or set up a payment plan at irs.gov/paymentplan. Send the response stub from the letter with your payment if instructed.
7. If you disagree: gather your documentation. This might be: a W-2 or 1099 you already reported, a receipt, a canceled check, or a corrected return from your tax preparer.
8. Write a response letter. Include: your name, SSN (last 4 digits on paper sent by mail), tax year, notice number, and a clear statement of why you disagree. Attach photocopies (never originals) of your supporting documents.
9. Send your response to the address on the letter. Use certified mail with return receipt. Keep the tracking number and a copy of everything you sent.
10. Wait. IRS processing times are slow — 30–120 days is normal. You'll receive a follow-up letter. If you don't hear back by their stated timeframe, call the number on the original letter.

Notes:
- Common notices and what they usually mean: CP14 = balance due; CP2000 = IRS received income you didn't report (or think you didn't); CP501/503/504 = escalating balance due reminders; LT11/Letter 1058 = final notice before levy. CP2000 is very common and often resolved by sending documents you already have.
- If you believe you don't owe what they say, respond in writing even if you're uncertain. Ignoring letters makes things worse — interest and penalties compound.
- The IRS will never initiate contact by phone, text, or email. If you get a call, it is a scam. The real IRS sends letters.
- If you can't pay the full amount, set up an installment agreement — this stops most collection action. You can do this at irs.gov without calling.
- Tax preparation services (H&R Block, local CPAs) will often handle IRS correspondence for a flat fee if you're overwhelmed. For complex cases, an Enrolled Agent (EA) is the most cost-effective specialist.
