HOW TO DEFER (POSTPONE) JURY DUTY

Steps:
1. Read the summons completely. Find: the reporting date, the court name, the juror ID or badge number, and the method for requesting a postponement (almost always online, by phone, or by mail with a specific deadline).
2. Understand that deferral and permanent exemption are different. Deferral means rescheduling to a later date. Exemption means being permanently excused. This procedure covers deferral. Most courts grant one deferral automatically.
3. Check the deadline to request a deferral. Most courts require you to request before your reporting date — often at least 7 days in advance, sometimes more. Some allow same-day requests for emergencies.
4. Request a deferral using the method listed on the summons. Online portals are the fastest and most common. You will enter your juror ID and select a new date from a list, or submit a reason and let the court assign one.
5. Common acceptable reasons for deferral (not permanent exemption): prepaid travel or vacation, scheduled medical procedure, work deadline with documented hardship, caretaking of someone with no other caregiver, school exams. Financial hardship alone is often grounds for deferral in many courts.
6. If you need a deferral beyond what the online system allows, write a brief letter to the jury commissioner. State your name, juror ID, reporting date, and the specific reason with supporting documentation (flight confirmation, doctor's appointment letter, etc.). Mail or fax it according to the summons instructions.
7. You will receive a new summons with a different date. Keep this. Do not assume you're done until you have written confirmation.
8. If you need to be permanently excused (not just deferred), this is a separate process requiring stronger documentation — active military deployment, medical inability to serve, financial hardship causing extreme difficulty (the bar is high). The process is the same but requires stronger supporting documents.
9. Show up on the rescheduled date. Ignoring jury duty entirely — even after a deferral request — can result in a fine or contempt of court order.
10. When you serve: bring your summons, government-issued ID, and the court's instructions. Most jurors are not selected for a trial. Most service ends in one day.

Notes:
- Federal and state courts have different processes. Your summons will clearly state which court it is.
- Being an employer does not exempt you. Being self-employed does not automatically exempt you either, though courts often show flexibility for sole proprietors.
- Courts will not exempt you for merely disliking jury duty, having opinions about the justice system, or wanting to avoid the inconvenience. They are familiar with all of these.
- Jury duty pay is low (typically $15–$50/day), but many employers are legally required to continue your salary during service — check your employment contract and state law.
- If your summons has passed and you missed it without a deferral: contact the court immediately, explain the situation, and ask to be rescheduled. Courts generally prefer to reschedule rather than enforce contempt proceedings for first-time non-responders who come forward proactively.
