Lineage

subtract did not originate. It recovers. The entries below are the works cited — the people and artifacts this project routes against, inherits from, and refuses to paper over.

Brian Fox — the substrate author

Born 1959. Started at the Free Software Foundation in 1985 working with Richard Stallman. Announced bash as a beta in June 1989 (Fox 1989a). Primary maintainer until early 1993. Along the way, authored the readline library, the history library, GNU Info, GNU Makeinfo, GNU Finger, and GNU Echo, and maintained GNU Emacs for a stretch.

Three things Fox wrote that subtract depends on directly:

bash itself. The dispatcher. command_not_found_handle. case. Parameter expansion. Process substitution. Every tier of subtract's routing is a bash construct Fox authored or an extension Ramey later added on top of Fox's foundation. subtract is GPL 3.0 because bash is GPL 3.0, and bash is GPL because Fox wrote it at FSF to be free in the anti-tivoization sense — not free as in gratis, free as in the authority layer cannot be captured.

readline. The library every interactive REPL sits on. Tab completion, Ctrl-R history search, line editing, keybindings. When T0 consults compgen for a completion candidate, the reason compgen has anything to say is that Fox made completion a first-class shell primitive in 1989.

the history library. !!, !$, !?pattern?, Ctrl-R, HISTFILE, HISTSIZE, history -w. This is episodic memory for the shell, shipped in 1989, present in every bash install since, forgotten by the generation that learned the word "memory" from LangChain.

Fox's later career is the same thesis applied in other domains. First interactive online banking software in the U.S. (Wells Fargo, 1995). Open-source election system (2008). Co-founder of the California and National Associations of Voting Officials. His 2017 New York Times op-ed with former CIA director R. James Woolsey (Woolsey and Fox 2017) is the clearest single statement of the forty-year thesis: the authority layer should not be proprietary.

Family footnote worth keeping: Fox is the grandson of Daniel Fox, who drew the Monopoly Man. The man who inherited the face of American capitalism's most recognizable mascot spent his career writing the free software substrate that capitalism's SaaS layer grew on top of and then paved over.

Chet Ramey — the substrate steward

Took over bash maintenance from Fox in 1993 and has held the seat for thirty-three years. His essay on bash in The Architecture of Open Source Applications (Ramey 2011) is the reference for how the shell is actually built, and the canonical source for why command_not_found_handle is a stable public hook rather than an implementation detail.

The lineage is: Fox wrote the substrate, Ramey kept it reachable. Neither claim without the other is the full story.

Marc Porat — the interaction model

Porat's Stanford dissertation (Porat 1977) is the first formal measurement of the information economy as a distinct sector. Two years later he co-wrote and hosted The Information Society (Hoffman 1980), the documentary containing the Johnny / rock-paper-scissors / neurosurgeon vignette that stated subtract's routing policy in eleven words forty-seven years before the router existed:

"Each one has strength. Each one has weakness. Only the way you choose is important."

Johnny grows up. Becomes a surgeon. The AI scrolls scan frames beside him while he cuts. Johnny grows old. The AI meets his granddaughter and teaches her the same game. One substrate, three generations, one lifetime.

subtract's five-tier router is the 1979 rock-paper-scissors speech restated in bash. Each tier has strength. Each tier has weakness. The dispatcher is only the choosing.

Yu-Jie Lin — the discovery pattern

March 2012: typing hel for tab completion, accidentally discovers help is a bash builtin. Blogs it (Lin 2012). July 2014: records a two-minute screencast walking through help help, help if, help for, help -m (Lin 2014). 256 views. 2016: stops blogging.

The capability was in the machine since before he was born. He found it by pressing tab. The video has 256 views because nobody is looking for the thing that was already there. The view count is not trivia. It is evidence that the pedagogical path to the substrate is broken.

subtract is the thesis that this is the normal case, not the exception. The tools were never lost. The path to them was.

Doug McIlroy — the philosophy

McIlroy invented the Unix pipeline in 1973. The | character. He also wrote the Unix philosophy: write programs that do one thing and do it well, write programs to work together, write programs to handle text streams because that is a universal interface.

subtract is the 2026 realization of McIlroy's vision. The LLM is just another text-stream processor in a pipeline. Intent in, command out, kernel executes. The architecture is a pipe.

Theo de Raadt — the trust primitive

OpenSSH is maintained by the OpenBSD project, founded by de Raadt. OpenBSD is famous for hostile code auditing and proactive feature removal. They improve security by deleting thousands of lines of bloated code.

They did not just build ssh-keygen -Y. They established the engineering culture of subtraction-equals-security that the governance layer relies on. The signed-frames architecture is OpenBSD philosophy applied to agent state.

John McCutchan & Robert Love — the event primitive

Before 2005, if a program wanted to know if a file changed, it had to poll the disk repeatedly, wasting CPU cycles. McCutchan and Love built inotify and merged it into the Linux kernel. User-space programs yield to the kernel and wake only when a filesystem event occurs.

Without their work, the governance loop would require a polling daemon or a heavy cron job to check for the governor's signature. inotify makes the zero-compute wait possible. The agent yields. The kernel wakes it. No polling.

Thomas Koenig — the deferred queue

While cron handles recurring schedules, at handles deferred execution. Pipe a command, give it a time, the kernel handles the rest. Koenig wrote the modern Linux atd daemon in 1993.

Every modern message broker, async task queue, and cloud function scheduler is a heavy, networked recreation of echo "command" | at now + 5 minutes. The primitive was always there. They rebuilt it with Redis and Celery because they did not know at exists.

The lineage, stated

McIlroy wrote the philosophy in 1973. Pipes. Text streams. One thing well.

Fox wrote the substrate in 1989 — shell, readline, history — and Ramey has kept it reachable for thirty-three years.

de Raadt and OpenBSD built the trust primitive and the culture of subtraction-equals-security.

McCutchan and Love built inotify so programs could yield instead of poll.

Koenig built at so tasks could defer without a message broker.

Porat imagined the interaction model in 1979. Lin demonstrated, with 256 views, that people will not find any of it without help.

Via negativa: we did not build this. We are pulling it out from under.
